english → kannada translator, inside the 8 dimensions

Model: attention-only encoder–decoder, dim=8, 4 heads (each head is exactly 2-D), 3,456 params. 8-D spaces are collapsed to 4 by PCA and drawn as x, y, z + color = 4th component. Marker symbol = input digit class (● 0, ■ 1, ◆ 2).

0 · the two languages, side by side

Kannada (right) has a real, finite alphabet: the 64 codebook words the decoder chooses from — these are the gray points of section 3. English (left) has no alphabet in this model: the encoder reads raw 4×4 patches as continuous vectors (an infinite vocabulary). The grid shown is a display-only k-means of english patches so you can compare the "letter shapes" of the two scripts. Note both are mostly blanks, edges and curve fragments — but curved differently.

2 · inside each head: no collapsing needed

dim 8 ÷ 4 heads = 2 dims per head, so each head's world plots directly. k = where a token stands to be found, q = what it looks for, v = what it hands over if attended to. Color = class. Watch which heads separate the classes and which only care about ink/position.

3 · decoder: how the kannada words are formed

Gray cloud: the 64 kannada vocabulary words as output directions (rows of the final head matrix, PCA→4; marker size = ink of the 4×4 patch the word paints). Colored path: the decoder's hidden state at each of the 49 painting steps (greedy) — the word whose direction it aligns with best is the word it says. Ring = word chosen at the current step. Variance: . Toggle to input embeddings to see the decoder's other space — the dictionary it reads back from (BOS in red). The two arrangements don't match each other, and neither matches pixel similarity: the dictionary is organized by usage, not by looks.
